#### Bulk vs Single-cell Sequencing
We've built these incredible machines but they've given data that has holes. For the last 20 years, bulk sequencing ~ the smoothie was the standard, it takes and blends this data into a goup, which is analyzed and usually tells you accurate information as it tells you the average compositon of the data. The smoothie is insufficent. Single cell sequencing ~ the fruit tart, instead of blending it, we chop it up and take out a single piece and we can tell the type of fruit it is, if it's a strawberry or kiwi. While we destroyed the cell, we have intact cells, but we had no idea that the strawberry was sitting right next to the kiwi. And in biology, who your neighbor is, is extremely important as cells talk to each other. A tumor cell functions differently if it's near a blood vessel or an immune cell. This brings us to spatial transcriptomics, we want to sequence the genes, but we want to know where they are in the tissue, we want the list of ingredients and a map.

#### Sparsity within the Data
Raphael is a computational biologist who looks at the raw data and points out that this map is mostly blank space ~ this is sparsity in a matrix of zeros where the rows are genes and the columns are locations in the tissue. This is a physics problem called capture efficency, where you're essentially fishing and dropping a molecular hook in to capture RNA molecules and you pick random fish and sometimes you just miss capturing a fish ~ this is a dropout event. When you see a zero in the data, you doubt about the confidence, is it a zero becasue the gene is actually off or is it because the machine missed it. In some of these technologies, you have a spot on the tissue that is only capturing potentially 20 transcripts, but in a cell there are hundreds of thousands of transcripts, only a small fraction of the biology is captured. We're basically seeing random pixels of the picture, but this isn't random, they belong to a structure— a geometry. The fruit tart has a strawberry that is part of a strawberry section, if we see a red pixel here and a red pixel there, we can infer that the space is probably red too. We're using spatial geometry to fill in the holes, we're going to stop treating the list of data as a string of numbers and treat it as a list of objects. And to do that, Raphael's team introduces three tools: Belayer, GASTON, and PASTE.

### Belayer
Belayer deals with the most common gemetric structure— the layer, like the layers of the skin or brain. The cerabal cortex for example has these layers and bands of distinct types of neurons. If you look at the gene expression in the cortext, it shouldn't be random, if you're in layer two, the genes should look like layer two genes. If you're standing in the custard layer, everything around me should be custard. Raphael's team thought, if we know the tissue is layered, we can use that information to fix the sparsity. If I see a spot that looks empty, but is sandwiched in between two spots that I'm sure are layer two, I can safely guess that the spot is layer two by borrowing information from the neighbors. 

![alt text](/My-Guide-To-Learning-from-Large-Scale-Biological-Data/Pictures/VideoSpatialT/VSpatialT-3.png)
#### Curves Layers + Heat Equations
Here is the catch, the cortex isn't a flat layer cake, they're folded, curved, and wiggles all over the place, so left might be a different layer if the tissue curves back on itself. We need a way to define depth in a curved object, this is where the physics and the first geometric leap comes in. Raphael asked, how do we naturally define distance in a curved shape? The answer~ the heat equation. Imagine the tissue slice is a piece of metal with the exact shape of the folded cortex and we attach a heater to the bottom layer and a block of ice to the top layer, now we just let the heat flow as heat diffuses moving from hot to cold. But, heat doesn't jump through empty space, it has to flow through the metal, it has to follow the curves, the geometry. The heat is tracing the shape of the tissue for us. If you solve the heat equation, which involves a mathematical operator called a laplacian, you get a map of the temperature of every single point in that tissue. Because it flows from bottom to top, the temperature tells you the depth. All the points at 5 degrees will form a line, a curved line that runs right down the middle of the tissue— a contour line. Another line is formed with all the 80 degree points~ these are called isotherms~ lines of constant temperature. These isotherms perfectly follow these biological layers. We're letting the shape dictate the grid. These for a conformal map.

#### Unrolling the Layers + Gradients
Once you have this temperature-depth coordinate, you can mathematically unroll the cortex, flattening out into a rectangle and the math becomes easy where you can look for patterns, you can look for genes that are on the hot zone and off in the cold zone. You can do even more, this is where Belayer really shines, you cen look for gradients, which is important because biology isn't always binary (on/off), so much developmental biology is about gradients, things called morphogens. You might have a gene that acts as a signal starting really strong at one side and slowly fading out moving across the tissue. Traditional clustering where you group cells into type A and B completely misses that, creating boundaries where none actually exist. It's like trying to draw a line between day and night, but there's a gradient. Belayer can see the twilight, because it has this continous depth coordinate from the heat equation, it can model a gene as a ramp sloping up or down, rather than a step function.

#### Results + Limitations
And the results speak for themselves, Raphael shows this method beating fancy deep learning models, he compares this to Graph Convolution Neural Networks (GCNs), which are a big deal in machine learning. The GCNs struggle because they're over-parameterized, they're trying to learn everything from scratch with no prior knowledge. Belayer is more simple, it knows that this is a layer, it just needs to find the curve, it imposes a geometric constraint that stablizes the answer and makes it more robust. But, there is a limitation, not everything is a layer such as tumors or livers, they're blobs. For these shapes, the analytic heat equation isn't enough. We need something more flexibly by moving from classical physics to modern AI— this is where GASTON enters. 

### GASTON
Belayer holds the rope, GASTON keeps this tension. GASTON is a neural network, but it's not a black box neural network. Raphael is quite critical about this trend in biology to throw data into a black block, but he wants interpretibility. 

![alt text](/My-Guide-To-Learning-from-Large-Scale-Biological-Data/Pictures/VideoSpatialT/VSpatialT-5.png)

#### Topographic Maps
GASTON is designed to learn a coordinate system just like Belayer did, but without being told ahead of time this is a layer. If we don't give it the heater and the ice, what is it optimizing for? It learns the latent map, specifically it learns to create a topographic map like hiking. He uses the example of the Hollywood hills. Imagine you're standing on a hill, you have your altitude~ your potential, and you have your slope, which way is up and down, whcih way is the steepest path~ the gradient. GASTON looks at the gene expression data and tries to find the shape that best explains how all the genes are changing across the tissue, it imagines the tissue is a landscape. So if gene expression changes really rapidly in one direction, that's a steep hill or cliff, and if it stays the same, that's a flat plateau, it's building a mountain range metaphorically, it creates isodepth lines which are just like the contour lines on a topographic map, lines of constant elevation and it creates vector fields. Similar to a weather map of arrows, these arrows represent the direction of change, the direction of the biological gradient. 

#### Composition vs State
This allows GASTON to solve a problem of composition vs state. Imagine you're studying coffee comsumption in a city, Coffee comsumption drops as you move from downtown center out to the suburbs. The gradient is high in the center, low on the outisde. There are two different possibilities for why there's this drop. Possibility A is people in the suburbs don't like coffee and prefer tea more, the people themselves are different— this is a change in cell type and composition. Possibility B: the people are the same downtown and in the suburbs who like coffee, but those in the suburbs are farther away from the good coffee shops, so they end up drinking less— this is a change in state, it's behavior has changed because of its environment. In biology, distinguishing between the two is a nighmare. If a gene turns off, is it because it died and was replaced by a different kind of cell or is the cell still there and it turned the gene off because of its neighbors or location. GASTON can tell the difference.

#### Example
Raphael shows an example with a mouse cerabelum. He looks at two cell types: Purkinje neurons and Bergman glia. GASTON allows them to mathematically separate these spatial effects, the change in shape from the cell type abundance, the change in composition. If you're trying to treat a disease, you want to know if the neurons are sick (state change) or if they're dead (composition change). GASTON gives you the mechanism, not just the observation by pointing you towards the why.

### PASTE2
We have Belayer for layers, GASTON for complex topographic maps, they're still an elephant in the room— the third dimension. Everything talked about so far is 2D, this leads us to the stacking problem. Usually if you want to build a 3D model, you stack up several layers like a loaf of bread that is sliced up and put back together. But, when you slice a biological sample, it distorts, streches, and tears and there's no guarentee about the orientation. So you need to align, position, and orient these layers to match. How do you know which layers to match. You can't just match by shape because shape changes slice by slice. You need an algorithm to align, not just by the outline but through its biological content, which is where PASTE2 comes in, probabilistical alignment of SRT experiments.

#### Optimal Transport
This dates back centuries, there was a french mathamician and military engineer named Gaspard Monge was trying to figure out the most efficent way to move a pile of dirt to fill a hole to build fornications, this is the optimal transport problem. You have a pile of dirt which is mass A and you have a hole which is mass B and you want to move the dirt into the hole with the least work (mass times distance). In Raphael's version, the dirt is the thousands of spots on slice A and the hole is the thousands of spots on slice B and he wants to move the spots from slice A to B. The cost is the biological distance as it costs a lot to align a liver cell to a blood vessel cell, you want blood to match with blood. PASTE looks for alignments that minimizes the movement and biological mismatch, it balances these two by finding the optimal transport plan. 
![alt text](/My-Guide-To-Learning-from-Large-Scale-Biological-Data/Pictures/VideoSpatialT/VSpatialT-10.png)
#### Initial Flaw
It allows you to stack the slices accurately to build that model but there was a flaw in the first version of the paper. The flaw was the assumption you need to move the whole pile of dirt. Munge assumed that all the dirt had to go into the hole and the hole has to be perfectly filled, which assumes that slice A and B are the same thing just moved or roated a bit. But in reality, if you tried to align the left and right slices of tumor but they only overlap by 10%. If you tried to force the left half to perfectly match the right half using the original optimal transport, you'd crush the data, you'd forcing things with no biological correspondence to match up and invent biology that isn't there. PASTE2 introduces partial optimal transport by allowing you to leave some dirt behind, the math get significantly harder as it aligns what it can and dumps the rest into the trash. The result is you can stitch together slices that barely touch. 

![alt text](/My-Guide-To-Learning-from-Large-Scale-Biological-Data/Pictures/VideoSpatialT/VSpatialT-11.png)
#### Example
They demonstrate this with a Drosphila embryo, they took 16 slices with sizes varying drastically from the head to the tail. PASTE2 could figure out this small ring corresponds to the center of this small ring and it could reconstruct the full 3D shape of the embryo. 

### Applications in Oncology
So, we've gone from correcting 2D sparse data with Belayer and GASTON to building full 3D atlases with PASTE2. The applications span into cancer. Ben Raphael is heavily interested in oncology, specifically tumor evolution. We hear a lot: the tumor developed resistance, and mutated and became more agressive. Traditionally we study that evolution by studying DNA and sequencing the genome of the tumor and look for mutations. Spatial transcriptomics measures the RNA, the output not the code itself, which as been a major diconnect in the field. 
![alt text](/My-Guide-To-Learning-from-Large-Scale-Biological-Data/Pictures/VideoSpatialT/VSpatialT-12.png)
#### Copy Number Abberations
Raphael found a proxy, a backdoor into the genome which is based on CNAs~ Copy Number Abberations. In cancer, the genome becomes extremely unstable where huge chunks of chromosomes get deleted or duplicated, so if you lose the DNA, it won't make the RNA, you can't make the smoothie without the fruit, creating these massive coordinates of silence in the signal. Raphaal compares it to walking into a library and the whole history section is gone, you don't need the list to notice something is gone, you can see the massive gap is gone. 
![alt text](/My-Guide-To-Learning-from-Large-Scale-Biological-Data/Pictures/VideoSpatialT/VSpatialT-13.png)
#### STARCH and CalicoST
Raphael's methods called STARCH and CalicoST scan the RNA data for these empty shelves as they're easy to spot in the noisy sparse data we started with. Since we already aligned these 2D slices with PASTE2, you can see the cells with the missing chromosome arms located in 3D space by mapping the clone. This give you a timeline of the tumor's evolution, a phylogeny, a family tree to track when this chromosome 1 loss started in the ancestor and the chromosome 6 loss is in the descendent. They did this with a patient's liver metastasis by taking slices and reconstructing it and identifying three distinct clones, they weren't all mixed up, and these regions of clones were clustered.
